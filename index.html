Who is Playing Storycraft?
Two distinct player profiles are emerging from the research.
The Curator
"I want to build a beautiful world"
  • Drawn to character creation and world customization
  • Uses story generation as a byproduct of crafting, not the goal
  • Plays like Animal Crossing — decorating, crafting
  • Represents the majority of players in the research
  • Risk: may never engage deeply enough with the core story mechanic
The Storyteller
"I have a vision and I'm executing it"
  • Comes in with a clear narrative idea and uses the game to bring it to life
  • Deeply engaged, high session time, high retention
  • Represents a smaller but extremely loyal segment
  • Risk: hits the game's current limitations faster — there is no end-game progression yet
Intended for storytellers. Loved by curators. The gap between them can be the opportunity.
D1 Retention Problem (30%)
"The onboarding is out of sequence. You ask so much work from the user before they know where they're going to land." — Iris, UX Designer & former Ubisoft onboarding specialist
Problem 1: The Promise Gap
App Store sets expectations onboarding doesn't deliver. Players arrive expecting world visits and social features that don't appear early enough.
→ Lead with the destination — show what a finished world looks like before asking for creative investment.
Problem 2: Character Roles Confusion
Every new user believed they were the main character, not the author.
→ The framing needs to be rethought from the ground up — not a tooltip fix, but a structural onboarding change.
Problem 3: Too Much Work Too Early
Character traits, author bio, world naming — all before the player has seen anything delightful.
→ Show the magic first, ask for the work second.
Problem 4: Traits Have No Perceived Value
Traits are discovered through conversation — but players don't understand what they do or why they matter.
→ Let traits emerge naturally from conversation — and make their impact visible. Players should feel the difference a trait makes, not wonder if it does anything at all.
Players are leaving before they get to the good part — and the onboarding is why.
What Loyal Players Are Missing
"When he can't decide what to do next, he leaves the game and opens Spin the Wheel — an external app where he's entered all his worlds and characters to pick his next goal." — Jon, 1+ year player, 40+ worlds built
Problem 1: Meaningless Progress
Leveling up happens regardless of what you build. Stephen said building anything advanced his level and his choices felt like they didn't matter.
→ Progression tied to meaningful creative milestones would change that.
Problem 2: No Celebration
When something is created, the game doesn't celebrate it. Alaria said "good, we got some points" — she didn't even know what points, she just wanted to feel progress.
→ Every creative milestone needs an emotional payoff. We designed Celebration to solve this — see the PRD and prototype at the end of this deck.
Problem 3: Direction Fatigue
Multiple engaged players rely on external tools — from deciding what to do next to generating better prompts.
→ Bring that scaffolding inside the product. An in-game creative assistant is the natural solution — already on the roadmap (ASAIK).
Problem 4: Stories Are One-Character Only
Each story is tied to a single character — two characters can't meet, interact, or conflict. For a storytelling game, that's a significant limitation.
→ Enable multi-character stories where characters from the same world can share scenes and shape each other's narrative.
Loyal players are filling the gaps themselves — others won't.
The Social Gap
The Storycraft community is real and passionate — but it lives on Discord, not in the game.
World Discovery — Inspiration Charts
Two natural motivations exist: creators want to show off their work, and players want to get inspired. Connecting these two is the social mechanic Storycraft is missing. The solution could be algorithmically curated ratings — Most Creative Characters, Most Inspiring Story, Hidden Gem — that create a discovery layer serving both.
Social Features Without Purpose
The game has social features — but players don't understand the value of using them. The community board is a good example: forced during onboarding, with no context of who sees it or why it matters. Social features only work when players understand what they get out of participating.
No Co-Creation
Multiple players expressed wanting to build something together with a friend. Right now that's not possible. Co-creation could be both a strong retention mechanic and a natural monetization opportunity — players investing in a shared world together.
Discord is doing the job the game should be doing.
Monetization Opportunities
Some players are engaged enough to pay. They just haven't been asked yet. Storycraft's monetization opportunity is unique — players don't buy content, they pay for better creative power. Every mechanic below is an extension of the core creation loop, not an interruption of it.
🎨
World Themes
Right now worlds look eclectic and mismatched — each item generated in whatever style the AI decides. A premium World Theme system lets players choose a visual style upfront (Dark Fantasy, Cozy Cottage, Cyberpunk) and all creations generate consistently within it. Players pay for coherence, not content.
⚡
Premium World Events
A 4th creation category beyond Item, Building, and Storage. AI-generated dynamic scenes that happen IN your world — a storm rolls in, a festival begins, a rival appears. Makes worlds feel alive and unpredictable rather than static displays.
👥
Multi-character Stories
Right now each story is tied to a single character — two characters can't meet, interact, or conflict. Premium unlocks the ability to write scenes across characters. Fixes a core product gap and monetizes the fix at the same time.
✨
Premium AI Generation
Basic generation stays free. Players who want more — richer story paths, more surprising chapter options, higher quality creations, more re-rolls — pay for better AI creative output. The free tier keeps the core loop intact. Premium unlocks creative power.
Players don't buy content. They pay for better creative power.
What I'd Fix First
Prioritized by impact on retention and monetization.
01
Redesign Onboarding
High Impact, High Effort
The goal is to get players to the core game mechanic as fast as possible. Hook players on the core loop early. Show the magic before asking for the work.
Target: D1 retention
02
Add Celebration Moments
Medium Impact, Low Effort — Quick Win
Every creative milestone needs an emotional payoff — the moment players feel the weight of what they just made. We went deep on this one. See the full PRD and prototype at the end of this deck.
Target: D7 retention + chapters completed per user
03
Build a Goal Map
High Impact, Medium Effort
Players shouldn't need an external app to decide what to do next. An in-game goal map with story paths and quests would reduce decision fatigue.
Target: Session frequency + session length
04
Launch Inspiration Charts
High Impact, Medium Effort
Creators want to show off. Players want inspiration. Algorithmically curated charts create a discovery layer serving both.
Target: Daily active visits + return sessions
05
Launch Premium Creation Tiers
High Impact, Medium Effort
World Themes, Premium World Events, Multi-character Stories, better AI generation. Players pay for better creative power.
Target: ARPU + conversion rate
What I'd Dig Into Further
Honest about what needs more time.
1
The Curator vs Storyteller Strategic Tension
The game is designed for Storytellers but most players are Curators. I'd want to understand whether the goal is to convert Curators into Storytellers, or to design a game that genuinely serves both. Those are very different product strategies — and the answer shapes everything else.
2
Monetization Timing and Triggers
When do players first feel the currency pinch? What triggers the first purchase consideration? I'd want to map the full economy curve before designing monetization features — getting this wrong is costly and hard to reverse.
3
The Social Asymmetry
Players want to be seen more than they want to engage with others. That needs a specific design response — not just more social features, but features that make giving recognition feel as rewarding as receiving it.
4
The Core Loop Is Too Rigid
Every chapter follows the same structure: choose a direction, complete 3 quests, each quest always requires creating an item. There's no narrative variety in the quest types themselves. A chapter about betrayal feels mechanically identical to one about discovery. This is worth rethinking.
